The Main Ethnicities

Malaysia consists of 3 main ethnicities, which are
the Malays, the Chinese and the Indians.

The Diverse Ethnicity

The people of Malaysia are unevenly distributed between Peninsular (West) Malaysia and East Malaysia, with the vast majority living in Peninsular Malaysia. The population shows great ethnic, linguistic, cultural, and religious diversity. Within this diversity, a significant distinction is made for administrative purposes between indigenous peoples (including Malays), collectively called bumiputra, and immigrant populations (primarily Chinese and South Asians), called non-bumiputra.

The Malays (Bumiputra)

The largest ethnic group in Malaysia, Malays are predominantly Muslim and form the majority population. They are culturally diverse, with influences from Malay, Indian, Chinese, and indigenous cultures. Malay language (Bahasa Malaysia) is the national language.

The Chinese

Chinese Malaysians are the second-largest ethnic group and have diverse cultural backgrounds, including Hokkien, Cantonese, Hakka, and others. They have contributed significantly to Malaysia's economic growth and are known for their entrepreneurship and cultural traditions.

  • Learn more

The Indians

Indian Malaysians comprise various ethnic groups such as Tamil, Telugu, Malayalee, and Punjabi. They have a rich cultural heritage including traditional dances, music, and cuisine. Many are descendants of laborers brought by the British during colonial times.

The Indigenous Group (Orang Asal)

There are 3 main types of Indigenous Peoples, namely the Orang Asli (from Peninsular Malaysia), Orang Ulu and/or Dayak (from Sarawak) and Anak Negeri (from Sabah). They constitute the Indigenous population of Malaysia.

Harmonious Coexistence

Malaysia's ethnicities represent the nation's strength, resilience, and identity, reflecting a shared heritage built on centuries of cultural exchange, interaction, and adaptation. The coexistence of multiple ethnicities within a single nation underscores Malaysia's commitment to fostering harmony, mutual respect, and understanding among its people. Embracing and celebrating this diversity is central to Malaysia's national identity and vision for a harmonious and prosperous future.
